abstract: >-
  <span class="lang-en">Designing a metamaterial for a target vibrational response is an inverse problem: many geometries satisfy one specification, and checking each one needs a slow finite-element solve. A practical design loop therefore needs two learned models, a fast <em>forward</em> predictor (geometry &rarr; response) and an <em>inverse</em> generator (response &rarr; geometry). Most deep-learning approaches keep this tractable by collapsing the response to a couple of band-gap numbers; this work builds the forward model that predicts the <em>entire</em> elastic band structure, the richer target a generator can later be conditioned on.<br><br>The unit cell is encoded as a graph, junctions as nodes, beams as edges, plus a few global descriptors. Node and edge features are lifted to a 768-dimensional space and refined by six edge-aware message-passing layers (GINE), so each junction gradually integrates its six-hop neighbourhood and effectively "sees" the whole cell. Ten learned <em>band queries</em> pool the nodes by attention, one query per band, into one embedding per band; a residual stack of 1D convolutions along the wavevector axis couples the bands and enforces smooth dispersion. A single forward pass returns ten bands over 311 k-points along Y-&Gamma;-X-M-&Gamma;, each with its frequency and a three-channel polarisation (one longitudinal, two transverse), replacing the per-design eigenvalue solve.<br><br>The key training idea: the simulator labels bands inconsistently across geometries (two near-identical cells can swap "band 7" after an avoided crossing), so a position-by-position loss would only teach the washed-out average. The loss is therefore permutation-invariant, an optimal band-to-band (Hungarian) assignment at every step, penalising the wrong <em>set</em> of curves rather than a relabelling. Shape terms (along-k correlation, slope, amplitude) keep the high bands from flattening, and a second head predicts polarisation on the same assignment.<br><br>With this forward model in place, the next step closes the loop: a graph VAE for on-demand inverse design (target band structure &rarr; geometry), and extending the approach to other physical properties.</span><span class="lang-fr">Concevoir un m&eacute;tamat&eacute;riau pour une r&eacute;ponse vibratoire vis&eacute;e est un probl&egrave;me inverse : de nombreuses g&eacute;om&eacute;tries satisfont une m&ecirc;me sp&eacute;cification, et v&eacute;rifier chacune demande un lent calcul par &eacute;l&eacute;ments finis.
