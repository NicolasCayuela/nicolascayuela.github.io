abstract: >-
  <span class="lang-en">A graph neural network that predicts the full elastic band structure of a beam-lattice metamaterial directly from its geometry, replacing the costly finite-element eigenvalue solves normally run for every new design. The unit cell is encoded as a graph: nodes are the lattice junctions, edges are the beams that connect them, plus a couple of global descriptors of the cell. Node and edge features are projected up to a 768-dimensional space and passed through six edge-aware message-passing layers (GINE), so each junction progressively gathers information from its six-hop neighbourhood and, in effect, "sees" the whole cell. Ten learned <em>band queries</em> then pool these node features by attention into one embedding per band (one query per band), and a residual stack of 1D convolutions along the wavevector axis couples the bands to each other and enforces smooth dispersion curves. The network outputs ten bands over 311 k-points along the Y-&Gamma;-X-M-&Gamma; path, each carrying its frequency and a three-channel polarisation (one longitudinal, two transverse). Training detail: because the simulator labels bands inconsistently across geometries (two near-identical cells can swap "band 7" after an avoided crossing), a per-position loss would teach the model to predict the washed-out average of those labels. Instead the loss is permutation-invariant: at each step an optimal band-to-band (Hungarian) assignment is found, so the model is penalised for the wrong <em>set</em> of curves, not for relabelling them. Shape-fidelity terms (along-k correlation, slope, and amplitude matching) sit on top to stop the high bands from collapsing to flat lines. A second head predicts polarisation, aligned to the same band assignment.
